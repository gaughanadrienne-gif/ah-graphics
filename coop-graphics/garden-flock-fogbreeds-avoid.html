Breeds to Think Twice About in the Santa Cruz Fog Belt

Not bad breeds overall -- just a poor match for our specific coastal conditions

White Leghorn Avoid
High egg production (280 to 320 per year) is genuinely impressive, but Leghorns have large, tall single combs that are among the most moisture-vulnerable of any common breed. They are also flighty and vocal, poorly suited to small lots with nearby neighbors. Their Mediterranean origins mean they were selected for heat dissipation, not damp tolerance.
Silkie Caution
Silkies are beloved for their appearance and gentle nature, but their feathers lack the interlocking structure of normal plumage and absorb moisture rather than shedding it. A Silkie in the fog belt gets soaked to the skin in damp conditions and is genuinely at risk for hypothermia. They also have a walnut comb, which is structurally fine, but the feathering issue makes them a poor fog-belt fit.
Cochin (Standard) Caution
Cochins have heavily feathered feet and legs that become mud-packed in wet ground conditions, which is a welfare and health issue. The feathered feet trap moisture and mud, increasing the risk of bumblefoot and frostbite on foot feathers. In a well-managed coop with dry bedding they can be kept here, but they require more active management than a fog-belt keeper wants.
Malay / Aseel Avoid
Tall game-type breeds bred for warm climates have minimal feathering and are not suited to cool, damp conditions. They were developed in tropical Southeast Asia for conditions that do not exist in the Santa Cruz fog belt.
Sultan Avoid
Sultans have feathered feet AND profuse head crest feathering that can obstruct vision and trap moisture around the face. Like Silkies, their unusual feather structure is not suited to damp conditions. They are ornamental birds that belong in a dry, protected environment, not the coastal fog.
Sources: The Livestock Conservancy Breed Profiles; American Poultry Association Standard of Perfection; UC ANR Small-Scale Poultry Flock (2023)
