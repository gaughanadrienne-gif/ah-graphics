Winter Flock Health: What to Watch and What to Do

Santa Cruz County specific (December through March)

Mud and Wet Litter

High risk in SLV and inland

Wet soil concentrates coccidia, bacteria, and ammonia. Spread coarse wood chips or rice hulls over run floor. Elevate feeders. Check coop floor stays dry -- wet bedding under roost bars is the biggest winter health threat.

Respiratory Disease

Watch entire county

Cold-season Mycoplasma and Pasteurella thrive in damp, poorly ventilated coops. Keep ventilation open above roost level. Early signs: bubbling eyes, gurgling breath, nasal discharge. Isolate and contact your vet promptly.

Parasites in Wet Soil

Inspect monthly

Northern fowl mites remain active in winter. Wet soil extends survival of intestinal parasite eggs. Check for mites under wing and at vent. If worm burden is suspected, consult your vet about fecal testing before treating.

  • Part feathers at vent area monthly
  • Inspect roost bars at night for red mites

Rodent Pressure

Rises Nov through Feb

Rain drives rats and mice toward coop warmth and feed. Store all feed in metal bins with lids. Use treadle feeders or remove feeders at night. UC IPM recommends sanitation over rodenticides near poultry to prevent secondary poisoning of birds.

Egg Production

Expect reduction

Natural light drives laying. At under 10 hours of daylight (Dec-Jan), many hens slow or stop. This is normal and beneficial: rest improves longevity. If you want winter eggs, add artificial light to bring total hours to 14-16. A simple timer and LED bulb work well.

Predator Risk Shifts

Verify hardware cloth

Coyote and raccoon activity increases in winter as natural food sources thin. Shorter days mean birds lock up earlier and earlier. Verify all hardware cloth is intact after wind and rain storms. Check aprons and under-coop gaps where digging predators probe.
