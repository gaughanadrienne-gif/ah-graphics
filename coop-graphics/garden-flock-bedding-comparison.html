Deep Litter vs. Sand: Coastal California Coop Bedding Comparison

Assessed for Santa Cruz County conditions: wet winters, marine fog, year-round humidity

Factor Deep Litter Sand
Wet winter performance (coop interior) Good when coop is covered and ventilated well Moderate; stays cool and damp in enclosed spaces
Wet winter performance (outdoor run) Poor in open runs; becomes anaerobic in heavy rain Excellent; drains fast, dries within 1-2 days
Ammonia control Excellent when managed correctly; microbes break down nitrogen Good if scooped frequently; poor if allowed to accumulate
Cleaning frequency Low; full cleanout 1-2 times per year Higher; scoop droppings every 1-3 days
Compost/garden value High; aged litter is excellent garden amendment Low; scooped sand has minimal soil-building value
Startup cost Low; wood shavings are inexpensive Moderate; coarse sand is heavier and pricier to haul
Summer warmth/coolness Slightly warmer (composting generates mild heat) Cooler; good for hot inland spells
Dust levels Moderate; dry bedding produces fine particulate Lower dust in the coop; no dry organic particulate
Mite and pest harbor Higher potential; organic material can harbor northern fowl mites Lower; mites do not thrive in sand
Suitability for ducks Challenging; ducks wet bedding rapidly with water spillage Much better; sand tolerates waterfowl moisture well
Dust bathing (chickens) Yes; hens dust-bathe readily in dry litter Yes; sand is an excellent dust-bathing medium
Source: UC ANR Small-Scale Poultry Management, 2022; UC ANR Poultry Health, 2021; Penn State Extension Poultry Bedding Management, 2023
