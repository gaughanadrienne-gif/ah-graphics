Santa Cruz County Flock Care Calendar

Key monthly tasks tuned to our coastal California climate

Winter

January

  • Mud management: add wood chips to run
  • Check ventilation; clear blockages
  • Inspect for mites and lice
  • Order spring chicks from hatchery

February

  • Deep-clean coop if not done in fall
  • Set up brooder equipment for chicks
  • Verify predator-proofing before breeding season
  • First chick arrivals (late Feb)

March

  • Chick season peak; brood indoors or in protected space
  • Egg production increases with longer days
  • Start supplementing calcium as laying ramps up
  • First spring parasite check
Spring

April

  • Move chicks to outdoor grow pen (weather permitting)
  • Manage garden-flock integration; fence vegetable beds
  • Inspect coop hardware cloth for winter damage
  • Coccidiosis risk rises with wet soil

May

  • Coast fog season begins; monitor humidity in coop
  • Peak laying for most adult hens
  • Transition pullets to layer or all-flock feed at 18 weeks
  • Check for mite infestations; treat if needed

June

  • Summer molt watch begins in older hens
  • Inland flocks: set up shade structures
  • Deep-clean waterers; heat accelerates algae
  • Monitor pullets for first eggs (if hatched in Feb-March)
Summer

July

  • Inland heat events: freeze water in treat dishes; add misters
  • Coast: fog keeps temps moderate; watch for respiratory issues from humidity
  • Keep feed fresh; summer heat turns feed rancid faster
  • Red mite pressure peaks; inspect roost bars

August

  • Watch for heat stress signs in inland flocks
  • Garden harvest season: safe scraps abundant
  • Predator pressure rises as coyotes and raccoons seek water
  • Lock-up times shift earlier; adjust evening routine

September

  • Fall molt begins in many hens (days shortening)
  • Increase protein: mealworms, sunflower seeds during molt
  • Egg production drops; this is normal
  • Begin annual coop deep-clean before rainy season
Fall and Winter

October

  • Deep-clean coop; refresh bedding before rains
  • Repair coop roof, hardware cloth, and vents
  • Full molt in progress; hens look rough (normal)
  • Add fresh bedding to roost areas for warmth

November

  • Rainy season arrives; mud management begins
  • Most hens resuming lay as molt concludes
  • Add supplemental light if desired (14 hours triggers laying)
  • Rodent pressure increases as rains push mice indoors

December

  • Shortest days; expect minimum egg production
  • Scratch grain before roost for overnight warmth
  • Monitor for respiratory illness in damp conditions
  • Plan spring flock additions; review breed notes
