Standard vs. Bantam Chickens: Small-Lot Comparison

Key factors for Santa Cruz County urban and suburban backyard flocks

Factor Standard (Large Fowl) Bantam
Hen weight 4 to 8 lbs depending on breed 1.5 to 2.5 lbs
Egg size Large to extra-large (56 to 70g) About half standard size (28 to 35g)
Annual egg output 200 to 280 eggs (productive breeds) 150 to 200 eggs
Coop space needed 4 sq ft per bird minimum 1.5 to 2 sq ft per bird
Run space needed 10 sq ft per bird minimum 4 to 5 sq ft per bird
Daily feed (per hen) 1/4 to 1/3 lb (about 1/2 cup) 1/8 lb (about 1/4 cup)
Flight ability Low to moderate; most cannot clear 3 to 4 ft High; many clear 5 to 6 ft easily
Fencing requirement 4 ft typically sufficient if clipped 6 ft or covered run recommended
Predator vulnerability Moderate (hawks take juveniles; dogs/raccoons are the main threat) Higher (small size attracts more hawk attention)
Cold and fog tolerance Generally good; large body mass helps Varies by breed; feather-footed types need dry conditions
Broodiness Varies; production breeds rarely go broody Many bantam breeds are reliably broody and excellent mothers
Temperament Generally calm; varies by breed Often bold and active; some breeds are very friendly
Garden impact Heavier scratching; more soil disturbance Lighter footprint; less digging damage
Noise Standard laying cackle; roosters audible to neighbors Slightly quieter; bantam roosters crow at higher pitch
Mixed-flock compatibility Compatible with other standard birds and large ducks Best housed separately or with care around large breeds
Source: Penn State Extension Poultry; UC ANR Poultry Management; Merck Veterinary Manual
