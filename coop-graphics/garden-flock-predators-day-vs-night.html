Santa Cruz County Flock Threat: Day vs. Night Quick Reference

Use this to prioritize your next defense improvement. Source: UC IPM; Cornell Lab eBird; CDFW.

Daytime Threats

Red-tailed Hawk Year-round resident. Targets young birds and bantams in open runs and pasture. Defense: covered run, remove elevated perches.
Cooper's Hawk Year-round resident. Can take birds up to 1.5 lbs. Ambushes through cover and run gaps. Defense: covered run overhead.
Sharp-shinned Hawk Winter visitor only (Sept through April). Threatens chicks and bantams only. Defense: covered run during winter months.
Domestic Dog Year-round. Multiple birds killed but none consumed. Ragged wounds across whole body. Defense: fully enclosed run; animal control report.
Coyote (active hours) Primarily nocturnal but increasingly active at dawn and dusk in suburban areas. Pup season (March through August) raises daytime sighting risk.
Bobcat Crepuscular (dawn and dusk) but will hunt at any hour. Present county-wide.

Nighttime Threats

Raccoon Obligate nocturnal. Dominant urban threat. Reach-through attacks. Can open latches. Defense: hardware cloth, locking latches, buried apron.
Great Horned Owl Year-round resident. Enters through unsecured vents. Decapitates birds. Defense: lock all birds in predator-proof coop every night.
Gray Fox Most frequently recorded wild mammal in Santa Cruz Mountains monitoring. Digs under and climbs. Defense: buried apron plus fence overhang or roller.
Long-tailed Weasel Confirmed at Bonny Doon Beach (2020). Enters through 1/4-inch gaps. Surplus kills entire flock. Defense: raised floor, 1/2-inch hardware cloth on every opening.
Opossum Strictly nocturnal. Targets eggs and nesting hens. Reaches through wire. Defense: 1/4-inch hardware cloth; no openings at roost level.
Rats Primarily nocturnal. Take eggs; bite toes of roosting birds; kill young chicks. Roof rat dominant species in coastal Santa Cruz. Defense: metal feed storage; seal all 1/4-inch or larger gaps.
Coyote / Mountain Lion Both primarily nocturnal. Mountain lion risk highest in San Lorenzo Valley. Defense: secure lockup at dusk; no birds outside after dark.
